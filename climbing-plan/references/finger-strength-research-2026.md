## Bottom Line

- Finger strength and finger endurance are strongly associated with climbing performance, but climbing performance is also technical, tactical, and recovery-dependent.
- Supplemental finger training can improve finger strength faster than climbing alone, especially over 8 to 10 week blocks, but the evidence base is still small.
- Capacity and repeatability are legitimate training goals, not a detour. When session quality is inconsistent or fingers get irritated after max attempts, build repeatable submaximal volume before chasing more max output.
- Max hangs are useful later, but they should be treated as high-intensity strength work, not background conditioning.
- Finger and pulley pain should guide load selection. Taping may reduce bowstringing after pulley injury, but it is not a strength fix and does not replace progressive load management.

## Evidence Summary

### Climbing Performance Depends on Finger Strength and Endurance

Saul et al. reviewed 32 prospective studies with 882 climbers and found that high-performing climbers tend to show better finger/forearm strength, endurance, postural control, movement efficiency, and recovery capacity. The review supports training finger strength and endurance, but also emphasizes technique and active recovery rather than pure strength alone. Source: [Determinants for success in climbing](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6527913/).

Stien et al. found in a systematic review and meta-analysis that climbing-specific resistance training improved climbing-related test performance compared with climbing alone. Reported effects favored finger strength, rate of force development, and forearm endurance, but the authors noted that climber intervention studies remain limited. Source: [Effects of climbing- and resistance-training on climbing-specific performance](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36636194/).

Langer et al. reviewed 12 controlled longitudinal strength-training studies in climbers. Their practical conclusion was that semispecific training such as fingerboard or campus-style work tends to be effective, and that combining static and dynamic work with maximum strength plus hypertrophy or endurance elements appears promising. Source: [Strength Training in Climbing](https://journals.lww.com/nsca-jscr/fulltext/2023/03000/strength_training_in_climbing__a_systematic_review.26.aspx).

### Hangboard Training Works, But Dose and Readiness Matter

Nord et al. ran a 10-week randomized controlled trial with intermediate to advanced climbers. Two weekly hangboard sessions added to normal climbing improved maximal finger strength more than normal climbing alone. The protocol deliberately started with two lower-resistance adaptation weeks before progressing. Source: [10 weeks hangboard training RCT](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2022.888158/full).

Mundry et al. compared hangboard protocols in intermediate to advanced climbers over 8 weeks. Adding weight progressively improved overall grip strength more than climbing alone, while the smaller-edge protocol did not clearly beat control overall. Source: [Hangboard training in advanced climbers](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8241953/).

Lopez-Rivera and Gonzalez-Badillo compared maximal hangs, intermittent hangs, and a mixed protocol over 8 weeks in advanced sport climbers. Grip endurance improved most in the intermittent-hang group, while max hangs also improved endurance by the end of the block. Source: [Three hangboard programs](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6458579/).

Stien et al. tested 5 weeks of dynamic finger flexor training in advanced to elite boulderers. Dynamic finger strength improved, but bouldering performance improved in both the training and control groups, so the direct performance transfer was less clear over such a short block. Source: [Dynamic finger flexor RCT](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2024.1461820/full).

Nelson et al. retrospectively analyzed logged training data and found that frequent low-intensity long-duration finger loading improved grip strength similarly to max hangs, with additive gains when both were combined. This is promising but lower-certainty than a controlled trial because the data came from app logs and training was not randomized. Source: [Different loading programs on finger strength](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-024-00793-7).

### General Strength Standards Support Progressive Loading

The 2026 ACSM position stand synthesized 137 systematic reviews and more than 30,000 participants. For voluntary strength, heavier loading at or above 80% 1RM, full range of motion, 2 to 3 sets, early placement in the session, and at least 2 sessions per week were associated with better gains. Hypertrophy favored higher weekly set volume, while power favored moderate loads moved fast. Source: [ACSM 2026 position stand](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41843416/).

Applied to climbing, max hangs fit the heavy-strength bucket. Capacity sessions, 5x5 board work, and intermittent hangs fit the repeatability and local muscular endurance bucket. Do not try to maximize both buckets in the same week when fingers are already irritated.

### Tendon and Pulley Evidence Supports Patient Load Progression

Bohm et al. reviewed 27 tendon-loading studies and found that tendons adapt to chronic mechanical loading, with loading magnitude especially important. Longer interventions, often beyond 12 weeks, appeared favorable for tendon adaptation. Source: [Human tendon adaptation meta-analysis](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-015-0009-9).

Larsson et al. systematically reviewed taping for pulley injuries. Evidence suggests taping can reduce bowstringing, but evidence for pain, return to sport, rupture prevention, and strength outcomes is weak or very weak. Source: [Pulley taping systematic review](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13102-022-00539-6).

Quarmby et al. systematically reviewed risk factors and injury prevention for overuse injuries in adult climbers. Higher climbing intensity, bouldering, crimp grip use, previous injury, and reduced grip/finger strength were associated with greater overuse injury risk. The authors highlight strength and conditioning, load management, and technique as prevention targets. Source: [Overuse injury risk factors in adult climbers](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38162697/).

El-Sheikh et al. found in a diagnostic review that dynamic ultrasound is recommended when pulley injury is suspected; clinical bowstringing alone is not enough. Source: [Diagnosis of finger flexor pulley injury](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2686056/).

## Planning Implications for This Athlete

Use this when the athlete is around V10 Kilterboard, aiming for V12, climbing 3 days per week, and reporting finger irritation after very hard attempts.

1. Start with a 3 to 6 week capacity block when sessions are inconsistent or fingers hurt after hard Kilter attempts.
2. Keep the main climbing work repeatable: 5x5-style board problems, submaximal links, and volume that preserves movement quality.
3. Keep finger pain during and after training at 0 to 3 out of 10. If pain is sharp, nervy, localized, worsening during the session, or worse the next morning, remove max hangs and hard crimping.
4. Use low-intensity finger loading only if it is pain-free: feet-on floor hangs, easy recruitment pulls, or easy open-hand loading.
5. Delay max hangs until at least 2 quiet finger weeks and consistent session quality.
6. When reintroducing max hangs, use 1 to 2 sessions per week, start conservatively, progress one variable at a time, and place them early after a thorough warmup.
7. Do not combine a new max-hang progression, hard V10+ board projecting, and high-volume crimp climbing in the same week.
8. Use intermittent hangs or repeaters later when the goal is forearm endurance or power-endurance, not when fingers are actively irritated.
9. If suspected pulley injury signs appear, stop hard crimping and recommend clinical assessment; dynamic ultrasound is the best-supported diagnostic tool in the reviewed literature.
10. Use taping only as support or proprioceptive feedback, not as permission to load harder.

## Practical Block Progression

### Capacity Block, 3 to 6 Weeks

- Board intensity: RPE 6 to 8.
- Main work: 5 problems x up to 5 clean repeats, or 4x4/5x5 links below max.
- Strength: 5x5 pull-ups, rows, dips, or core at moderate effort.
- Finger loading: easy recruitment only, no true max hangs.
- Progression: cleaner repeats, shorter rest, or one slightly harder problem, not all at once.

### Max Strength Block, 6 to 10 Weeks

Use only when fingers are quiet.

- Finger work: max hangs or edge pulls 1 to 2 times per week.
- Intensity: heavy but controlled; stop before form breakdown or pain.
- Climbing: fewer desperate board attempts on max-hang weeks.
- Progression: add small load or one set only after completing the plan without pain.

### Power-Endurance / Project Block, 4 to 8 Weeks

Use after capacity and max-strength base are stable.

- Main work: hard links, repeaters, interval bouldering, and project sections.
- Intensity: one high-intensity finger stressor per session.
- Guardrail: if finger pain rises above 3 out of 10, return to capacity-style climbing for 1 to 2 weeks.

## Confidence and Gaps

Confidence is medium. The direction is consistent across reviews and trials, but climbing intervention studies are still small, often short, and use different tests, grades, and protocols. The strongest practical rule is not a single perfect protocol; it is matching the training block to the athlete's current tissue tolerance and recovery.
